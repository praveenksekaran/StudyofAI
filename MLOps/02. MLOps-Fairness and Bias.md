# Responsible AI for Developers: Fairness & Bias

## Introduction to Responsible AI: Course Notes

### 1. The Landscape of AI Responsibility
* **Ubiquity of AI:** AI is integrated into daily life (traffic, weather, recommendations, generative AI).
* **The Infallibility Gap:** No AI model has 100% accuracy. Failures occur when systems are used in real-world contexts that differ from their training environments.
* **Under-specification:** Models may perform well during training but fail to be robust or fair in new, unpredictable situations.

### 2. The Human Element in AI
* **Human-Centric Decisions:** Machines do not make the primary decisions; people do. 
* **The ML Lifecycle:** Humans are involved at every stage:
    * Data collection and creation.
    * Model design and building.
    * Deployment and application control.
* **Value Embedding:** Every decision point (e.g., choosing generative AI over other methods) reflects the developer's personal or organizational values.

### 3. Defining Ethics in AI
* **Ethics vs. Law:** Law and policy are informed by ethics, but most ethical norms are not yet codified into law.
* **Three Pillars of Ethics:**
    1.  **Prescriptive:** What we *ought* to do, regardless of common practice.
    2.  **Accountability:** What others can rightly blame us for not doing.
    3.  **Social Flourishing:** A tool for living well together as social creatures.
* **Proactive Stance:** Organizations cannot wait for global consensus or laws to be written; they must help shape the norms.

### 4. What is Responsible AI?
* **Definition:** An approach aimed at developing AI ethically by understanding possible issues, limitations, and unintended consequences.
* **Framework:** There is no universal checklist. Organizations must develop foundational principles that reflect their specific mission.
* **Common Themes:** Across most organizations, four themes recur:
    * **Fairness**
    * **Interpretability**
    * **Privacy**
    * **Safety**

### 5. Benefits of Responsible AI Development
* **Trust:** Building responsibility into the deployment process creates trust with customers. Without trust, adoption stalls.
* **Accountability:** Constant evaluation (of both successes and failures) ensures products are beneficial to everyone.
* **Innovation:** Considering ethical constraints empowers developers to find new, creative ways to achieve a mission without causing harm.
* **Risk Mitigation:** Prevents model misuse (e.g., fraud/voice mimicking) and protects stakeholders from harmful outcomes.

## Google's AI Principles

### 1. Overview of Approach
* Google’s AI principles act as concrete standards governing research, product development, and business decisions.
* Responsibility is incorporated "by design" into both products and the organization.
* The approach is rooted in four pillars: building for everyone, maintaining accountability and safety, respecting privacy, and driving scientific excellence.

### 2. Principle 1: Bold Innovation
AI should be developed to assist, empower, and inspire across all human endeavors.
* **Benefit vs. Risk:** Deploy models only where likely benefits substantially outweigh foreseeable risks.
* **Scientific Advancement:** Use the scientific method and rapid iteration to accelerate breakthroughs in fields like biology, medicine, and physics.
* **Real-World Impact:** Focus on solving global challenges and making breakthroughs broadly available to help humanity achieve ambitious goals.

### 3. Principle 2: Responsible Development & Deployment
Responsibility must be maintained throughout the entire lifecycle, from design and testing to iteration.
* **Mindful Design:** Mitigate potential harms from the beginning of the development process.
* **Human Oversight:** Implement feedback mechanisms and due diligence to align with social responsibility and human rights.
* **Safety and Security:** Pioneer technical solutions to address risks and share safety benchmarks with the broader ecosystem.
* **Rigorous Testing:** Use monitoring and safeguards to avoid unfair bias and unintended harmful outcomes.
* **Protection:** Prioritize privacy, security, and respect for intellectual property rights.

### 4. Principle 3: Collective Benefit and Empowerment
AI is a collective journey; tools should be made to empower others to innovate.
* **Foundational Technology:** Develop AI as a platform that drives creativity and productivity across various fields.
* **Stakeholder Collaboration:** Engage with academia, industry, governments, and civil society to address challenges no single entity can solve alone.
* **Ecosystem Growth:** Foster a vibrant environment that enables others to build their own innovative tools and contribute to the field.

## Responsible AI Practices

Developing responsible AI requires blending general software best practices with considerations unique to machine learning.

### 1. Human-Centered Design
The impact of AI is best assessed through actual user experience.
* **Disclosures and Control:** Build features with clear disclosures to ensure users have clarity and control.
* **Augmentation vs. Automation:** Consider suggesting multiple options to a user rather than a single prediction, as precision is often higher for a set of answers.
* **Iterative Testing:** Model adverse feedback early; test with a small fraction of live traffic before full deployment.
* **Diversity:** Engage diverse users and scenarios to incorporate a wide variety of perspectives.

### 2. Multi-Metric Assessment
Avoid relying on a single metric to understand trade-offs between different errors.
* **Diverse Data Points:** Use user surveys, overall system performance quantities, and product health indicators (e.g., click-through rate vs. long-term customer value).
* **Contextual Appropriateness:** Align metrics with system goals (e.g., a fire alarm system should prioritize high recall/sensitivity over avoiding false alarms).
* **Subgroup Slicing:** Track performance across different user subgroups to ensure fairness.

### 3. Raw Data Examination
Models directly reflect their training data; therefore, data must be analyzed carefully.
* **Privacy-First Analysis:** If raw data is sensitive, use aggregate anonymized summaries to understand inputs.
* **Accuracy and Representation:** Ensure data is free of errors (missing values, wrong labels) and represents real-world settings.
* **Minimize Bias:** Ensure the dataset is fairly representative of the entire population to avoid skewed outcomes.
* **Simplicity:** Remove redundant features and avoid unnecessary model complexity.

### 4. Awareness of Limitations
Understand and communicate what the model and dataset *cannot* do.
* **Correlation vs. Causation:** Avoid mistaking patterns for cause-and-effect (e.g., buying basketball shoes does not *cause* someone to grow taller).
* **Scope Communication:** Inform users about the training set’s scope (e.g., a model trained on stock photos may struggle with mobile phone photos).
* **User Education:** Clearly state limitations to improve user feedback and manage expectations.

### 5. Rigorous Testing and Quality Engineering
Borrow from traditional software engineering to ensure the system is trustworthy.
* **Unit and Integration Tests:** Test individual components in isolation and as part of the larger system.
* **Detecting Input Drift:** Monitor data distributions to ensure they haven't changed unexpectedly since training.
* **Poka-Yoke (Mistake-Proofing):** Build quality checks so that failures trigger an immediate response (e.g., if a feature is missing, the system should not output a prediction).
* **Gold Standard Datasets:** Use and regularly update a high-quality dataset to verify the system behaves as expected.

### 6. Continuous Monitoring and Updating
Deployment is not the end of the lifecycle; models require ongoing maintenance.
* **Real-World Feedback:** Use happiness tracking surveys and frameworks (like HEART) to monitor performance.
* **Roadmap Planning:** Build time into the product roadmap to address the fact that all models are imperfect.
* **Candidate Analysis:** Before updating a model, analyze how it differs from the current version and how it will impact user experience.

## Case study: Google Flights
The Google Flights team utilized machine learning to demystify complex flight pricing, focusing on saving users time, stress, and money while maintaining transparency.

### 1. Challenges in AI Price Prediction
Machine learning predictions are not 100% accurate due to two primary factors:
* **Specificity:** Predictions are highly specific to certain flights, destinations, and times.
* **Data Scarcity:** Limited pricing data can make it difficult to provide a perfect "fair price" prediction.

### 2. Design Principles for Price Intelligence
To ensure user trust, the team established three core design principles:
* **Honest:** Providing realistic assessments rather than "salesy" conclusions.
* **Actionable:** Giving users information they can use to make a booking decision.
* **Concise yet Explorable:** Offering a simple summary while allowing users to dig into the underlying data.

### 3. Explaining ML Outputs to Users
The team translated complex model outputs into user-friendly components:
* **Price Goodness Indicator:** Categorizes current prices as "high," "typical," or "low".
* **Usual Price Explanation:** A single-line summary of what a similar trip typically costs.
* **Likelihood Predictions:** Simple text stating if prices are "likely to go up" or "not likely to go down".
* **Data Source Transparency:** An info icon providing a bubble that explains which data sources were used for the insight.

### 4. Key Strategies for Responsible Implementation
* **Confidence Thresholds:** The team moved away from specific percentages (e.g., 75% chance) because they were confusing. They pivoted to only showing predictions with **90% confidence or higher**.


* **Articulate Data Sources:** Telling users where data comes from avoids "contextual surprises" and privacy concerns, helping users know when to apply their own judgment.
* **Early User Research:** Frequent testing helped the team realize that a "simple conclusion" (e.g., "Today is a good day to book") felt untrustworthy to users, leading them back to a more transparent approach.

### Summary
Building responsible AI requires a repeatable process where decisions—from design to deployment—are evaluated for their impact on user trust and the system's overall usefulness.

# Fairness & Bias

## Overview of Fairness & Bias

### 1. Understanding Bias
Bias is defined as stereotyping, prejudice, or favoritism toward specific individuals or groups. While not all biases are harmful, they are often difficult to recognize because they are embedded in our personal perspectives and cultural backgrounds.

### 2. Common Types of Human Bias in AI
There are over 100 types of cognitive biases. The five most common that manifest in machine learning include:

#### 1. Reporting Bias
*   **Definition:** When the frequency of events in a dataset does not reflect real-world frequency.
*   **Cause:** People tend to document the unusual or memorable and omit the "ordinary."

#### 2. Automation Bias
*   **Definition:** A tendency to favor results from automated systems over manual ones, regardless of their error rates.

#### 3. Selection Bias
Occurs when data is not representative of the real-world distribution. Forms include:
*   **Coverage Bias:** Non-representative data selection.
*   **Non-response Bias:** Gaps in data due to lack of participation.
*   **Sampling Bias:** Lack of proper randomization during collection.

#### 4. Group Attribution Bias
The tendency to generalize individual traits to an entire group.
*   **In-group Bias:** Preference for members of one's own group.
*   **Out-group Homogeneity Bias:** Stereotyping outsiders as being "all the same."

#### 5. Implicit Bias
Assumptions based on personal mental models that don't apply generally.
*   **Confirmation Bias:** Favoring information that affirms pre-existing beliefs.
*   **Experimenter’s Bias:** Continuing to train a model until it produces the result the researcher expects.

### 3. The AI Lifecycle and Bias Entry Points
Bias is rarely caused by a single factor; it is often a systematic error introduced at various stages:
*   **Data Collection:** Wrong assumptions or poor sampling procedures.
*   **Training:** Models find and repeat biased patterns found in the input data.
*   **Logic Definition:** Bias in how model components and mathematical logic are defined.
*   **Post-Deployment:** Feedback loops and iterative updates can introduce or amplify bias over time.

### 4. Defining AI Fairness
Fairness is more restrictive than bias. A system is considered **unfair** if decisions are based on **sensitive variables** such as:
*   Gender or Sexual Orientation
*   Ethnicity
*   Disability

**The Goal:** Ensure AI systems are fair and accessible at scale, especially as they move into critical tasks like job matching, fraud detection, and safety.

### 5. Challenges in Achieving Fairness
Achieving perfect fairness is difficult due to several factors:
*   **Data Amplification:** Models may amplify existing societal biases found in historical data.
*   **No Universal Definition:** Fairness is subjective and depends on social, cultural, legal, and ethical contexts.
*   **Metric Incompatibility:** Different mathematical definitions of fairness often contradict each other; satisfying one may violate another.
*   **Contextual Necessity:** Fairness must be defined specifically for each individual AI problem rather than using a "one size fits all" approach.

#### What-If Tool
<img width="367" height="306" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/ec288a93-c30f-447b-b0ba-22360bc70730" />

*   **Counterfactual Analysis:** Allows users to modify specific features (e.g., changing "male" to "female") on individual data points to see if the model’s prediction "flips," helping identify hidden biases.
*   **Flip Ratio Metric:** Quantifies how often changing a sensitive feature across the entire dataset alters the model's output, measuring the model's vulnerability to bias.
*   **Partial Dependence Plots:** Provides visualizations showing the correlation between single features (like age or education level) and the model's prediction to detect learned prejudices.
*   **Distance-Based Comparisons:** Enables finding the "nearest neighbor" (most similar data point) using L1 or L2 distance to compare two examples side-by-side and highlight feature differences.
*   **Performance Slicing & Constraints:** Allows for group-based analysis of confusion matrices and the automatic calculation of optimal classification thresholds to meet fairness goals like **demographic parity**.
